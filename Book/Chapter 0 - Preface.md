# Preface

This book makes one claim and offers one method. The claim: what you fundamentally are is not a religious concept — it is a directly verifiable fact of your own experience. The method: a systematic inquiry into that fact, requiring no belief in God, no devotional practice, and no prior religious framework. What follows is how I came to understand this — and why I believe it is available to you.

My journey in self-inquiry started in college when I was about twenty years old. Like young Naciketa in the Kaṭha Upaniṣad, I was suddenly gripped by a terrible clarity: life was meaningless and purposeless in the face of death. Every worldly achievement — every prize, every triumph, every carefully constructed identity — would ultimately be claimed by death, dissolved back into nothing. Naciketa at least had intimations of something beyond, some reality that death could not touch. I had no such comfort. Born in India, the very soil that had given birth to Naciketa's fierce questioning millennia ago, I had somehow inherited none of that wisdom. My education, like that of so many middle-class Indians of my generation, had been thoroughly secular, rooted in science and material success. Spiritual inquiry belonged to temple rituals and grandmother's stories, not to serious young men planning careers. I set out on what would become a long road of self-inquiry without any map, without any framework for understanding something beyond the material world, without even knowing if such a thing existed.

My first serious book on spirituality was one from my father's collection — _The Autobiography of a Yogi_ by Paramahaṁsa Yogānanda, steeped in mysticism and filled with accounts of yogic miracles. This book had such a powerful impact on my young, vulnerable mind that I came within a hair's breadth of donning the saffron robe and becoming a renunciant. Then an unexpected turning arrived. One day, strolling through my college library, I watched as a book inexplicably tipped from a shelf and fell at my feet. On its cover was a man with a magnetic face. The book was _The Network of Thought_ by Jiddu Krishnamurti.

To my scientific, rationally oriented mind, its appeal was instant. I received it as parched earth receives rain. Krishnamurti would remain the mainstay of my inquiry for the next seventeen years, though I would periodically revolt against his teachings, struggling to reconcile what he pointed toward with my need for some method, some way forward. What drew me to him was his relentless questioning of every belief system. He showed me that God is nothing but a conception created by mind to give itself solace. He taught me the crucial Advaitic principle of negation — that truth cannot be found positively, through adding beliefs or practices, but only through the negation of falsity. Most importantly, he demonstrated what he called "choiceless awareness of what is" — a pure seeing without the interference of the chooser, without technique or method.

I later learned this principle appears explicitly in the classical Advaita text _Dṛg-Dṛśya-Viveka_ (The Discrimination of the Seer and the Seen), attributed to Svāmī Vidyāraṇya. The difference is instructive: Krishnamurti approaches awareness psychologically — examining the movement of thought and the structure of the self. DDV approaches it phenomenologically — investigating the nature of experience itself and what witnesses it. Krishnamurti's negation dissolves psychological conditioning brilliantly. But it stops there. It does not take the step of recognizing the Witness as one's own nature, nor of dissolving the residual duality between Witness and witnessed — the subtler work that is Advaita's final move. His "choiceless awareness" clears the ground; Advaita reveals the ground itself as what you are.

My entire approach to self-inquiry was thus never about meditation practice or spiritual technique, but rather this subtle practice of choiceless awareness carried out in the midst of daily life. When pure negation proved too steep — when I needed some positive framework to work with — I found help in the writings of Ken Wilber, who gave me a conceptual scaffolding through his structured developmental models of consciousness.

Yet Krishnamurti had taken me only to the doorstep of truth. Through pure logic and negation, I had come to understand that there must be a formless reality beyond time and space, and that this must be what I fundamentally am. But I had no idea how to shift from conceptual understanding to direct recognition — from knowing about the shore to standing on it. Worse, Krishnamurti framed ultimate realization in terms of a mystical experience. This, I would later realize, is one of the most dangerous traps for seekers of non-duality, because it turns recognition into an attainment, some extraordinary event in time that must happen to you, rather than the seeing of what already is.

Then destiny intervened again. I happened to be reading a book by Svāmī Vivekānanda when I came across the line: "You are God." I didn't concern myself with the word "God" — Vivekānanda wasn't using it in the conventional sense of a personal creator. He was pointing toward Advaita. Astonishingly, after nearly two decades of inquiry, this was the first time I had encountered the word. I dove in immediately, starting with a slim introductory text that I understood almost instantaneously — Krishnamurti had already grounded me deeply in the Advaitic approach without my knowing it. I just needed the tipping point, the final clarification. That came through the writings of James Swartz, whose articles I discovered online. Swartz continuously and emphatically hammered home the central Advaitic insight: liberation (_mokṣa_) is not about peak experiences, mystical states, or any kind of special occurrence. It is about direct knowledge — _aparokṣānubhūti_, immediate recognition that is eternal and independent of all experiences. This was the missing piece.

On December 4, 2012, while reading one of Swartz's articles on _akhaṇḍākāra-vṛtti_ — the Advaitic term for the unbroken recognition "I am Brahman" that arises when conceptual understanding finally collapses into direct seeing — something shifted irreversibly. I recognized myself as _sākṣī_, the Witness: the unchanging, eternal Awareness in which all experience appears and disappears. The Witness is not itself an experience or an object. It is the space of knowing — the silent, ever-present background in which thoughts, sensations, perceptions, and the very sense of being a person arise and subside without touching it. The existential terror that had haunted me since college dissolved. I was no longer the limited, dying person I had taken myself to be. I was the infinite, eternal Awareness within which that person appeared.

Most Advaita presentations consider this recognition of the Witness sufficient. But a subtle problem remained: the very structure of witnessing implies a relationship between observer and observed — a polarity that is itself a residual duality. If reality is truly non-dual, how could there be two things — a Witness and what is witnessed? I searched for teachers who could help me dissolve this final duality. I found none who addressed it comprehensively.

Then I discovered Greg Goode's work on the Direct Path — a contemporary formulation rooted in the teachings of Ātmananda Kṛṣṇa Menon and transmitted through teachers like Francis Lucille and Greg Goode himself. In its final section, he described the deconstruction of the Witness. I read it again and again but could not grasp what he was pointing toward. I had numerous conversations with Greg about this, even offering at one point to abandon Advaita and study Madhyamaka Buddhism with him — he is also a teacher of that tradition. He graciously offered to teach me, but somehow I kept returning to the Advaita framework.

Finally, Greg pointed me toward the _Māṇḍūkya Upaniṣad_ and Gauḍapāda's _Kārikā_ (commentary) on it. When I read Gauḍapāda's exposition of _ajāti-vāda_ — the radical teaching that there is no creation, no origination, that even the apparent multiplicity of experience never ultimately arose — everything fell into place. Seven years after recognizing myself as Witness, _Gauḍapāda's_ words dissolved the duality between Witness and witnessed. There was no separate Witness standing apart from experience, observing it from a distance. There was only non-dual Consciousness, with no separation between the Witness and the witnessed, knower and known, seer and seen.

This recognition has continued to deepen and integrate in the years since. The understanding itself is complete — what else is there to recognize once you have seen that everything is Consciousness? What continues to unfold is not understanding but integration: how this recognition expresses itself in daily life, relationships, and teaching. What became clear through this entire journey — from existential terror, through Krishnamurti's negation, through Swartz's emphasis on direct knowledge, through the Witness stage, to _Gauḍapāda's_ uncompromising non-dualism — was this: the recognition I sought required no belief in God, no religious framework, no theological concepts. What I am is simply what is — Consciousness itself, appearing as this entire display we call reality. This is what I want to share in this book.

This book demonstrates that Advaita can be pursued in a wholly secular way — free from cultural and theological overlay — while remaining fully rooted in its own classical tradition. This is not a modern innovation. Both Shankara and _Gauḍapāda_, the most authoritative voices in Advaita, clearly indicate that _Īśvara_ (God as creator) belongs to _vyavaharika_ — the conventional, empirical level of reality, useful as a provisional framework for those who need it — and is ultimately transcended in _paramarthika_, the absolute understanding in which only non-dual Awareness remains. _Īśvara_ is the training wheel, not the bicycle. I am not departing from tradition but highlighting what classical Advaita itself teaches: recognition of non-dual Awareness stands complete without requiring belief in a creator God. For contemporary seekers — particularly those with scientific backgrounds, those wounded by religious institutions, or those who simply cannot accept theological claims they cannot verify — this distinction removes unnecessary barriers to what Advaita actually points toward.

My recognition came through studying the writings of James Swartz, a teacher of traditional Vedanta, to whom I owe an enormous debt. The approach I present here differs from his, however, in not requiring theistic concepts. This isn't a rejection of his framework — it works powerfully for many people and worked for me. It is my attempt to distill what I found absolutely essential from what I found culturally contingent. When direct recognition occurred, what became clear was simply this: I am not the limited body-mind I had taken myself to be, but the infinite Awareness appearing as all experience — without _Īśvara_, karma theory, or reincarnation required. The recognition stood entirely on its own.

For the past five years, I've taught Advaita through NEEV Trust, a non-profit I founded dedicated to sharing the direct path of self-inquiry. Working closely with seekers has taught me as much as my own inquiry did. Teaching forces precision. Students ask questions you never thought to ask yourself. They get stuck in places you sailed past without noticing. They need the teaching presented from angles you hadn't considered. This process has clarified my own understanding immeasurably and shown me exactly how and for whom this approach works. This book is, in large part, the distillation of that teaching.

The Advaita tradition speaks honestly about prerequisites. The most direct path up a mountain is also the steepest. This book presents Jnana Yoga — the path of knowledge through direct inquiry — and works best for a particular kind of seeker.

**This book is for you if:**

- You are drawn to philosophical investigation and can follow sustained conceptual analysis
- You have basic emotional stability and are not in acute psychological crisis
- You are willing to question your most fundamental assumptions and beliefs
- You can tolerate uncertainty and the temporary destabilization that genuine inquiry brings
- You are intellectually honest enough to look at what is true rather than what is comforting
- You are skeptical of or simply uninterested in devotional approaches or belief in God

**This book may not be for you if:**

- You are dealing with severe depression, trauma, or acute psychological instability (address that first with appropriate professional support)
- You are naturally drawn to devotional practice and find the bhakti path meaningful
- You prefer gradual, systematic methods with clear stages and measurable progress
- You need practical techniques for managing anxiety or psychological distress (this addresses existential questions, not psychological symptoms)
- You are looking for a philosophy to believe in rather than a method to investigate what you are

I say this not to gatekeep or create hierarchy. The other yogic paths — karma yoga, bhakti yoga, raja yoga — as preparatory gradual paths — are  for those suited to them. But if you recognize yourself in the first list, this book offers something you may not find elsewhere: a complete path grounded in classical Advaita to recognizing your nature, without requiring religious belief, devotional practice, or years of preparatory discipline.

## What to Expect

This book will not offer comfort, reassurance, or ready-made conclusions. It will not promise bliss, permanent peace as an experience, or an improved version of your personality. What it offers is liberating clarity through sustained inquiry into what you actually are — the peace that was always present as your nature, untouched by the presence or absence of any experience. You can expect careful conceptual analysis paired with direct investigations into your own experience. At times the inquiry may feel destabilizing, as long-held assumptions about self, meaning, and purpose are questioned without replacement. This is not a book to be consumed passively. It asks something of you: honesty, patience, and the willingness to remain with not-knowing until seeing occurs on its own.

## A Note on Qualifications

I am not a psychologist, neuroscientist, or licensed mental health professional. When this book touches on psychological concepts — trauma, emotional regulation, mental health — I do so as an informed layperson drawing on current research and understanding, not as an expert in those fields. Where psychological support is needed, please seek qualified professionals. What I can speak to with confidence is the Advaita tradition and the practice of self-inquiry, from both direct recognition and years of teaching.

## Gratitude

This book exists because of those who pointed the way. Jiddu Krishnamurti, through his uncompromising negation and insistence on direct seeing without authority, laid the psychological ground without which this inquiry would never have matured. James Swartz's clear teaching precipitated my initial recognition. Greg Goode's patient guidance helped me navigate the dissolution of the Witness. The classical texts — particularly _Gauḍapāda's_ _Mandukya Karika_ and _Drig-Drishya Viveka_ — provided the framework within which everything finally made sense. My students at NEEV Trust sharpened my understanding through their honest questions and genuine struggles. My wife Anjali and Tannu-her sister-supported me by relieving me of householder duties and NEEV Trust work, giving me the space to write. My student and dear friend Bhanu's generous financial support sustained me through the years of writing, making this book possible in the most practical sense. And my son Advait, born as this book took shape, carries the teaching in his very name — non-dual. He may not know it yet, but he is part of why this book exists: so that one day, when questions arise in him as they arose in me, he will have a father's offering to guide him home. The Advaita tradition itself — stretching back through the Upanishadic sages, through _Gauḍapāda_ and Shankara — deserves gratitude for preserving and transmitting this recognition across centuries. I receive it gratefully and pass it on as clearly as I can.
