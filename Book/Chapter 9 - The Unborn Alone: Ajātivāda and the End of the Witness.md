There is a moment in the inquiry when the ground you thought you were standing on turns out not to have been ground at all. Chapter 8 brought you to the recognition that the world you perceive has no independent substance — that what appears is vivartavāda's teaching made experiential: apparent modification without real transformation, the universe as illusory superimposition upon the unchanging screen of Awareness. This was not a small recognition. It dissolved the tyranny of objecthood, the compulsive conviction that things exist as densely, separately, threateningly as they appear. The Witness stabilized. There was Awareness here, appearances there, and the appearances had no power over what they appeared in.

But a residue remained: Awareness here, observing objects there. Subject and object, even if the objects had been seen through as illusory. The Witness position — however refined, however released from identification with the body-mind — still preserved the grammar of duality. Knower and known. Seer and seen. Observer and the observed.

Advaita insists: not-two. 

Gaudapāda, the 6th–7th century Advaita philosopher whose Māṇḍūkya Kārikā this chapter unfolds, diagnosed this residue with a precision that no subsequent teacher has surpassed. His diagnosis was not that the Witness had the wrong objects. It was that the Witness rested on a false assumption: that objects had ever been produced — even illusorily. Vivartavāda taught that the universe appears without really transforming Brahman. Ajātivāda retracts even this concession. Nothing appears. Nothing was ever produced. Not really. Not apparently. Not in any sense in which the word 'production' can coherently be applied.

This is ajātivāda — the doctrine of non-origination. Ajāti: non-arising. It is the philosophical culmination toward which every previous chapter of this book has been moving, whether or not that was visible at the time. The Dṛg-Dṛśya-Viveka, which occupied Chapter 8, was never the final word. It was the necessary penultimate step: establishing the Witness cleanly, burning the vāsanās that would obstruct recognition, preparing the ground for a recognition more radical than any the text itself contains. The Māṇḍūkya Kārikā asks the question the DDV does not: not 'what is the Witness observing?' but 'were the objects ever actually there?'

When that question is answered — truly answered, not merely understood as a philosophical position — the Witness position loses its foundation. Not because something new has been attained. But because the logical ground for the observer-observed structure is seen to have been absent all along. What remains is not Awareness having absorbed the world. It is Awareness that never moved, never divided, never produced anything: unborn, whole, the sole reality it has always been.

This chapter unfolds in three movements: The first movement — Śravaṇa — introduces the Māṇḍūkya Upaniṣad and Gaudapāda's Kārikā, then surveys all the creation theories that Gaudapāda systematically examines, showing how their mutual contradictions inadvertently disclose what Gaudapāda calls the one non-contradictory position: non-origination. The second movement — Manana — takes up the rigorous logical analysis of cause and effect in all its possible forms, addresses the sharp objections this position generates, and presents Gaudapāda's firebrand analogy as the experiential illustration of a world that appears without ever being produced. The third movement — Nididhyāsana — draws the philosophical thread to its conclusion: if nothing was ever produced, the Witness has no object; and if the Witness has no object, the Witness position itself dissolves. What remains is not dissolution into void. It is the recognition of what the tradition calls sahaja — the natural state — Brahman alone, as it always was.

## 1. Śravaṇa: The Text and Its Central Claim

### 1.1. The Māṇḍūkya Upaniṣad and Gaudapāda's Kārikā

A clarification of texts is necessary before we proceed, because confusion on this point leads to systematic misreading of what follows.

The Māṇḍūkya Upaniṣad is one of the shortest of all the Upaniṣads — twelve terse verses. Its subject is the syllable AUM and the four states of consciousness: waking (jāgrat), dream (svapna), deep sleep (suṣupti), and the fourth — Turīya — which is not a state at all but the substratum of all states. The text ends with a declaration of breathtaking economy: Turīya is that which is not perceived internally, not perceived externally, not perceived from both sides, not a mass of consciousness, not conscious, not unconscious. It is unseen, unrelated, ungraspable, without inference, unthinkable, indescribable — the very essence of the knowledge of the one Self. The world ceases in it. It is peaceful, auspicious, non-dual. This fourth is the Self. This should be known.

This is the Upaniṣad itself. What this chapter primarily draws upon is different: Gaudapāda's Kārikā, a philosophical commentary on the Upaniṣad composed in four chapters (prakaraṇas). The first chapter — Āgama Prakaraṇa — establishes the four states and draws its conclusions from scriptural authority. The second — Vaitathya Prakaraṇa — demonstrates the unreality of the waking and dream worlds through philosophical argument. The third — Advaita Prakaraṇa — presents the non-dual nature of consciousness. The fourth and most philosophically ambitious — Alaṭaśānti Prakaraṇa, the Chapter on the Quenching of the Firebrand — contains the systematic logical deconstruction of causation that constitutes ajātivāda proper.

When this chapter quotes 'the Kārikā', it is Gaudapāda's own verses that are being cited. When it quotes Śaṅkara, it is the bhāṣya — the commentary — that accompanies those verses. This distinction matters because Gaudapāda's argument is Gaudapāda's argument: not the Upaniṣad's, though fully consistent with it. It is one of the most sustained and rigorous pieces of philosophical reasoning in the entire Sanskrit tradition. It deserves to be engaged on its own terms.

The central thesis of the Kārikā, stated in its most concentrated form, is this: nothing has ever come into being. Brahman — pure Consciousness, the Self — is unborn (aja), unmoving, undivided. Whatever appears to have been born, to have moved, to have become diverse, has never actually done any of these things. This is not an empirical claim about what scientists have discovered about the cosmos. It is a philosophical conclusion arrived at by subjecting every conceivable theory of origination to rigorous logical scrutiny, and finding each one incoherent.

Before the deconstruction, however, comes the survey.

### 1.2. The Survey of Creation Theories

Gaudapāda opens his inquiry by cataloguing the theories that different schools and thinkers have proposed to explain why anything exists at all. He is not engaging in comparative religion or intellectual history for its own sake. He has a specific philosophical purpose: to show that the theories, taken together, are mutually inconsistent in ways that reveal something important about the nature of the question itself.

The theories surveyed in the first chapter of the Kārikā can be organized into two types: teleological theories, which explain creation in terms of its purpose, and cosmological theories, which explain it in terms of its mechanism.

### 1.3. The Creation Theories — Kārikā 1.6–1.9

Verses 6 through 9 of the first chapter survey the field with remarkable compression:

**Kārikā 1.6**

*svapnamāyāsvarūpau vā jīvau prajñāpatiṃ prati | utpannāviti caikasya dvitīyaṃ na tu vidyate ||*

*Creation is the manifestation of the divine power of God; or again it is like a dream or illusion. It proceeds from God's mere will, say the wise. Time alone is the womb of all beings, say the thinkers of time; it proceeds from the enjoyment of God, say others; it is the sport of God, say still others; but all this is merely the nature of the Effulgent Being — for what desire can exist in Him whose all desires are ever in a state of fulfillment?*

The verse surveys what Śaṅkara in his bhāṣya enumerates as six distinct theories: (1) creation as manifestation of God's divine power; (2) creation as dream or illusion, manifested by God; (3) creation as the expression of God's omnipotent will; (4) creation as proceeding from Time (Kāla), with Īśvara uninvolved; (5) creation for the purpose of God's enjoyment; (6) creation as God's sport (līlā). These are not fringe positions — they represent the full range of Hindu theological cosmology, from the realistic Naiyāyika view to the Sāṃkhya materialist account to the devotional Vaiṣṇava theology of līlā.

**Kārikā 1.8–1.9**

*icchāmātraṃ prabhoḥ sṛṣṭiriti sṛṣṭau viniścitāḥ | kālātprasūtiṃ bhūtānāṃ manyante kālacintakāḥ || bhogārthaṃ sṛṣṭirityanye krīḍārthamiti cāpare | devasyaiṣa svābhāvo'yamāptakāmasya kā spṛhā ||*

*Those who affirm the existence of created objects attribute this manifestation to the mere will of God, while those who look upon time as real declare time to be the manifestor of all beings. Others think that manifestation is for the purpose of the enjoyment of God, while still others attribute it to mere diversion. But it is the very nature of the Effulgent Being — for what other desire is possible for Him whose desires are always in a state of fulfilment?*

Gaudapāda's reply to all of these, contained within the final line of verse 9, is elegant and devastating in equal measure. Creation cannot be for God's enjoyment or sport, because Brahman — whose desires are always in a state of fulfilment — has no unsatisfied want that could motivate the act of creation. A will that creates must be a will that lacks. Brahman lacks nothing. The cosmological and teleological theories therefore collapse together: they attribute to Brahman a condition — want, incompleteness, motivated purposiveness — that is incompatible with Brahman's nature.

And yet Gaudapāda does not stop here to declare victory. He recognizes that the theories are not simply wrong in a careless way. They arise from genuine inquiry into genuinely puzzling phenomena. The world does appear to exist. The question of why it exists is a real question — real, at least, from within the assumption that it does exist. Gaudapāda's deeper strategy becomes clear only in the fourth chapter, where he will question that assumption itself. For now, the survey has served its first purpose: establishing that no creation theory accounts for creation without attributing to the ground of being properties that contradict its nature.

### 1.4. The Contradictions as Disclosure

The Alaṭaśānti Prakaraṇa opens its survey of creation theories at a more technical and more philosophically pointed level. Here the contenders are specifically the Sāṃkhyas and the Naiyāyikas-Vaiśeṣikas — the two dominant schools of pre-Advaita Indian philosophy — and the contradiction between them is the precise instrument Gaudapāda deploys.

The Sāṃkhya position holds that an effect pre-exists in its cause (satkāryavāda): the pot is latent in the clay before the potter shapes it; oil pre-exists in the sesame seed before pressing releases it. By 'birth' the Sāṃkhya means manifestation of what was already there. By 'destruction' it means return to the causal source. Nothing genuinely new is produced; nothing genuinely ceases.

The Naiyāyika-Vaiśeṣika position holds the opposite (asatkāryavāda): the effect is genuinely new, not pre-existing in the cause. Something comes into being that was not there before. The pot is a genuinely new entity, not merely clay rearranged.

These two positions — existence from existing, existence from non-existing — represent the exhaustive logical alternatives. Every possible creation theory must hold one or the other, or some combination. And yet each refutes the other conclusively:

**Kārikā 4.3–4.4**

*dravyato'nye dravyato'nye pravadanti vicakṣaṇāḥ | vikurvanti tathā'nye'nye janayanti tathaiva ca || bhūtaṃ na jāyate kiṃcidabhūtaṃ naiva jāyate | vivadanto dvayā hyevamajātiṃ khyāpayanti te ||*

*The wise disputants differ regarding the nature of things — some think they arise from existing substance, some from non-existing substance; some think things change, others that they are produced. The existent cannot again pass into birth as an existence. Nor can the non-existent be born or come into being as existent. Thus disputing among themselves, they, as a matter of fact, tend to establish the Advaita view and support the Ajāti — the absolute non-evolution of what exists.*

The argumentative structure here deserves careful unpacking, because it is the logical spine of the entire chapter.

If the effect already exists in the cause (Sāṃkhya), then there is no production — the effect merely becomes manifest. But then 'production' is not production in any meaningful sense. Nothing genuinely comes into being. The Naiyāyika seizes on this: if the effect already exists, why call its appearance a birth at all?

If the effect does not pre-exist in the cause (Naiyāyika), then something comes into being from absolute non-being. But how can non-being produce being? The Sāṃkhya seizes on this: a non-existent cause, like the horns of a hare, can produce nothing.

Each school's objection to the other is valid. The Sāṃkhya successfully demolishes the Naiyāyika's production-from-nothing. The Naiyāyika successfully demolishes the Sāṃkhya's production-that-isn't-production. What this mutual demolition reveals, Gaudapāda observes with a philosophical irony that is almost playful, is that they have together argued for what neither of them intended: that production, in any coherent sense, is impossible.

**Kārikā 4.5**

*khyāpyamānāmajātiṃ tairanumodāmahe vayam | vivadāmo na taiḥ sārdhamavivādaṃ nibodhata ||*

*We approve the Ajāti — the non-creation — declared by them. We do not quarrel with them. Now hear from us the Ultimate Reality which is free from all disputations.*

This verse has a quality that might be mistaken for intellectual generosity but is actually something more precise. Gaudapāda is not saying 'everyone has a piece of the truth.' He is saying that the dualist schools, by successfully refuting each other, have jointly proved a position neither of them holds. Their argument, taken to its logical conclusion, establishes ajātivāda. The Advaitin does not need to fight anyone. The opponents have done the work already.

The contrast with the Advaitin position is stated with equal precision:

**Kārikā 3.18**

*advaitaṃ paramārtho hi dvaitaṃ tadbheda ucyate | teṣām ubhayathā dvaitaṃ tenāyaṃ na viruddhyate ||*

*As non-duality is the Ultimate Reality, therefore duality is said to be its effect. The dualists perceive duality either way — both in the Absolute and in the phenomena. Therefore the non-dual position does not conflict with the dualist's position.*

The dualists quarrel with each other because each takes the relative level to be the ultimate one, and at the relative level their positions are genuinely contradictory. The Advaitin does not quarrel because, from the standpoint of ajātivāda, duality at the conventional level is precisely what one would expect if there is, at the ultimate level, only unborn Brahman. The appearance of duality does not contradict non-duality; it is what non-duality looks like from inside ignorance.

### 1.5. The Provisional Status of Creation Teaching

Before proceeding to the logical analysis of cause and effect, one further point that Gaudapāda makes in the first and third chapters must be registered. He explicitly addresses why creation theories are taught at all, if non-origination is the ultimate truth.

The answer is pedagogical and compassionate, not evasive.

**Kārikā 3.15 (Śaṅkara bhāṣya)**

*Creation has been described in order to help the understanding of the student to grasp the unity of Ātman. It is like the story of Prāṇa — described not because it is ultimately real, but because describing it serves the student's progressive understanding.*

The Āgama Prakaraṇa verse on the three grades of intellect (Kārikā 3.15 and the associated commentary) elaborates this directly: there are students of low, middle, and high intellectual capacity. Those of low capacity look upon the phenomenal universe as real. Those of middle capacity worship the causal Brahman — Brahman as the ground of creation — and remain on what Śaṅkara calls 'the causal plane.' Only those of high capacity are capable of grasping the non-dual Ātman directly. For the first two categories, the Scripture, out of compassion, teaches creation — the realistic theory for those at the first level, the dream-illusion (vivartavāda) theory for those at the second. These teachings are not false in the sense of being carelessly wrong. They are provisionally true: true enough to move the student forward, true at the level where the student currently operates, but ultimately superseded.

This is why Chapter 8's work — establishing vivartavāda, burning vāsanās, stabilizing the Witness — was not wasted effort. It was the necessary second stage for minds that had outgrown simple realism but were not yet ready for the full negation of ajātivāda. The present chapter is the third and final stage, appropriate for those whose intellect has been sharpened by the preceding inquiry and who are now capable of hearing — without retreating into consoling middle-ground positions — that no creation has ever occurred.

With this pedagogical context established, we can turn to the argument itself.

## 2. Manana: The Deconstruction of Cause and Effect

### 2.1. Every Theory of Causation Fails

The Alaṭaśānti Prakaraṇa's logical core is a systematic examination of every possible relationship between cause and effect. Gaudapāda's strategy is not to argue for any particular positive theory. It is to show that each possible model of causation, subjected to rigorous examination, collapses. The argument has a cumulative logic: each examined model failing increases the pressure of what remains — until what remains is only the recognition that causation itself is incoherent, and therefore that creation is impossible.

#### 2.1.1 **The Identical-Cause Argument**

The first major argument concerns the case where cause and effect are identical in nature. This is the Sāṃkhya position: the effect is non-different from the cause; the pot is pre-existent in the clay.

**Kārikā 4.11**

*asato māyayā janma tatvato naiva jāyate | vandhyāputro na tattvena māyayā vāpi jāyate ||*

*If the effect is the same as the cause, then the effect also becomes unborn. Further, how can the cause be permanent if it be non-different from the effect which is born?*

The argument cuts both ways. If cause and effect are identical, then either the effect is as unborn as the cause (in which case no production has occurred), or the cause is as born as the effect (in which case the cause is not a permanent, unborn ground). The Sāṃkhya cannot have both: an unborn, permanent cause and a genuinely produced effect that is non-different from it. The identity relation forces a choice that destroys the theory whichever way it is made.

#### 2.1.2. The Different-Cause Argument

If cause and effect are different in nature — the Naiyāyika position — a parallel problem arises:

**Kārikā 4.13**

*ajādvai jāyate yasya dṛṣṭāntastasya nāsti vai | jātācca jāyāmānasya na vyavasthā prasajyate ||*

*There is no illustration to support the view of him who says that the effect is born from an unborn cause. Further, if it be contended that the effect is born from a born cause, then that cause must be born from some other born cause and so on — an infinite regress from which we never reach an unborn ground.*

The Naiyāyika cannot point to a single example in which something genuinely new has arisen from an unborn source — because every example involves a cause that was itself produced. Trace any causal chain backwards and you face infinite regress: there is no first cause that is itself uncaused. The attempt to ground production in an unborn first cause is philosophically empty unless one can actually point to such a cause — and one cannot.

#### 2.1.3. The Immortal-Mortal Problem

Verses 4.6 through 4.9 press a deeper difficulty: the impossibility of a change of nature.

**Kārikā 4.6–4.7**

*ajātasyaiva dharmasya jātimicchanti vādinaḥ | ajāto hyamṛto dharmo martyatāṃ kathameṣyati || na bhavatyamṛtaṃ martyaṃ na martyamamṛtaṃ tathā | prakṛteranyathābhāvo na kathaṃcidbhaviṣyati ||*

*The disputants contend that the ever-unborn, changeless entity undergoes a change. How does an entity which is changeless and immortal partake of the nature of the mortal? The immortal cannot become mortal, nor can the mortal ever become immortal. For, it is never possible for a thing to change its nature.*

This argument has a deceptive simplicity that conceals its depth. If Brahman — the supposed ground of creation — is immortal, unchanging, undivided, then it cannot produce anything that is mortal, changing, and divided, because that production would constitute a change in Brahman's own nature. A thing cannot be simultaneously what it is and what it is not. The changeless cannot change. The immortal cannot produce the mortal. If it could, it would not have been changeless and immortal to begin with. Every creation theory must explain how the timeless becomes temporal, how the infinite becomes finite, how the undivided becomes multiple. And this explanation, whenever pressed to its logical terminus, amounts to asserting that the ground of being has contradicted its own nature — which is incoherent.

#### 2.1.4. The Impossibility of Sequencing Cause and Effect

Verses 4.18 through 4.21 introduce a further difficulty that strikes at the structural logic of causation itself: the impossibility of establishing which comes first, cause or effect.

**Kārikā 4.18–4.19**

*jātaṃ na jāyate kiṃcidajātaṃ jāyate katham | jāyamānaṃ na jāyeta kuto jātiravāstavaḥ ||*

*If the cause is produced from the effect and the effect is again produced from the cause, which of the two is born first, upon which the birth of the other depends? The inability to reply, the ignorance about the matter, and the impossibility of establishing the order of succession of cause and effect — these clearly lead the wise to their theory of absolute non-evolution.*

This argument has the structure of what logicians call a vicious circularity. If you need the cause to explain the effect, and need the effect to identify what counts as a cause, you have no non-circular starting point. The standard response — the illustration of the seed and the sprout — is addressed directly:

**Kārikā 4.20**

*bījāṅkurākhyo dṛṣṭāntaḥ sadā sādhyasamo hi sa |*

*The illustration of the seed and the sprout is always the same as the major term to be proved — that is, it itself needs to be established. It cannot be admitted in support of a contention that is itself unproved.*

**Kārikā 4.21**

*pūrvāparāparijñānam ajāteḥ paridīpakam |*

*The ignorance regarding the antecedence and the subsequence of the cause and the effect clearly proves the absolute non-evolution of things.*

The inability to establish the temporal priority of cause over effect — which is the very logical backbone of the causal relation — does not merely reveal ignorance about a fact. It reveals that the causal relation itself has no coherent content. A cause that cannot be identified as prior to its effect is not functioning as a cause. Causation requires asymmetric temporal sequencing: the cause precedes, the effect follows. When this asymmetry cannot be established, not even in principle, the concept of causation has been emptied.

At this point — after surveying the identical-cause failure, the different-cause failure, the immortal-mortal impossibility, and the sequencing incoherence — the cumulative argument has reached its conclusion:

**Kārikā 4.55–4.56**

*yāvaddharmaphalaprāptiryāvaccādhyātmadarśanam | tāvannānātvam āyāti kṣīṇe nānātve na kiṃcana || yāvaddhetuphalāveśaḥ saṃsārastāvadāyataḥ | kṣīṇe hetuphalāveśe saṃsāraṃ na prapadyate ||*

*As long as there is faith in causality, the endless chain of birth and death will continue. When that faith is destroyed by knowledge, birth and death become non-existent. All this is seen to be born on account of the illusion of experience due to Avidyā — therefore nothing is permanent. Again, as one with the Ultimate Reality, all this is nothing but the unborn Ātman.*

The destruction of faith in causality is not a psychological event. It is a logical one: you see, with the precision of a philosophical proof, that causation as a category is incoherent. Not difficult to apply. Not limited in scope. Incoherent. And if causation is incoherent, then creation — which is causation applied to the origin of the world — has never occurred.

### 2.2. The Deconstruction of Memory - Logically and Phenomenologically

There is a thread that has been running beneath the argument of Chapter 8 that must now be pulled to the surface and examined. The vivartavāda account of the world's appearance — which Chapter 8 deployed as its working framework — required a mechanism. If the world is not independently real, if it is a superimposition of name and form upon the unchanging screen of Awareness, something must explain why the superimposition happens at all, and why it has the particular content it does. Why does this body appear and not some other? Why does this history seem to precede this moment? Why does the same person seem to wake each morning into the same story?

The traditional answer — and the correct provisional answer — is vāsanā: the stored impressions of past experience, lodged in the causal body, projecting themselves as the apparent world of waking and dream. The causal body is the reservoir; vāsanās are its contents; the world of experience is their projection. This is a coherent and pedagogically useful account. Chapter 8's nididhyāsana — the burning of vāsanās from the Witness standpoint — was premised on it. The student was taught to watch from the Witness position as phenomena arose and passed, allowing the Witness's non-reactivity to thin the impressions that fuelled the projection. The account worked. The vāsanās thinned. The Witness stabilized.

But vāsanā, as a causal mechanism, depends entirely on one thing: the reality of memory. Vāsanā means impression — the residue of a past experience that persists through time, accumulates, and conditions future experience. For vāsanā to be a real causal mechanism, there must be a real past that deposited real impressions that are really being carried forward into a real present. The entire architecture of the causal body, of saṃsāra understood as a causal chain running through lives, of karma as the accumulated weight of past action — all of it stands or falls on the coherence of memory as genuine access to a real past.

Ajātivāda does not merely chip away at this edifice. It removes the foundation. If causation is incoherent — and Gaudapāda has shown, verse by verse, that it is — then the causal mechanism of vāsanā is itself part of the appearance, not the explanation of it. The provisional teacher who offered vāsanā as the mechanism of world-projection was not lying. The account was true at the level at which it was offered — the level of the student who still needed a mechanism, who was still operating within the causal plane. But ajātivāda is the recognition that even the mechanism was never real. The causal body, the vāsanās, the stored impressions, the chain of cause and effect running from past experience to present appearance — these are themselves part of what was never produced.

Here the Kārikā makes a move that repays the closest attention. Before deconstructing causation in the abstract, Gaudapāda tracks exactly how memory functions as the internal glue of the imagined saṃsāric sequence.

#### 2.2.1. **Gaudapāda's Account: Memory as the Engine of Saṃsāra**

**Kārikā 2.16**

_jīvaṃ kalpayate pūrvaṃ vaco bhāvānpṛthagvidhān | bāhyānādhyātmikāṃścaiva yathāvidyastathāsmṛtiḥ ||_

_First of all is imagined the Jīva — the embodied being — and then are imagined the various entities, objective and subjective, that are perceived. As is one's knowledge, so is one's memory of it. These are followed by their memory, which in its turn is followed by further states of knowledge. In this way are imagined various objects, subjective and objective, which are perceived and seen to be related to one another as cause and effect._

Śaṅkara's commentary on this verse, drawing on Ānandagiri's gloss, is luminously precise about the mechanism. The saṃsāric loop, as Gaudapāda maps it, runs as follows: knowledge of an experience produces a memory similar to that knowledge; that memory produces further knowledge; further knowledge produces further memory; and from this self-reinforcing sequence arises the entire world of subject-object relationship — agents and their actions, causes and their effects, a perceiver and the perceived. The loop is not driven by objects in an external world pressing themselves on a passive mind. It is driven by memory. Memory is the engine of the appearance of continuity, of personal history, of a world that seems to persist from moment to moment.

The crucial observation Śaṅkara makes in his bhāṣya deserves to be held: knowledge and memory form a mutually conditioning pair. Knowledge gives rise to a memory similar in character to the knowledge; that memory in turn generates further knowledge of the same character. What appears as the experienced world is not a sequence of independently existing events. It is a sequence of thoughts and their memories, presenting themselves as a world with causal texture. The external world that seems to confront the perceiver is, on Gaudapāda's account, memory-shaped thought — thought that has organized itself into the apparent form of an independent reality.

This is vivartavāda's inner mechanism exposed. And it is precisely this mechanism that ajātivāda must deconstruct. The Kārikā does so in two moves that operate at different levels: first, logically, by showing that the causal structure within which memory operates is incoherent; second — and here Gaudapāda's resources require supplementation — by pointing to what memory actually is when examined as a present-moment phenomenon rather than as a theoretical mechanism.

#### 2.2.2. **The Kārikā's Logical Deconstruction of Memory's Claims**

The Kārikā's logical deconstruction of memory is indirect but decisive. Memory claims to be access to a real past. For that claim to be coherent, the following must be true: there was a real past event; that event produced a real impression in the causal body; that impression is being genuinely carried forward in time; and the present memory is a genuine retrieval of that stored impression. Each of these steps is a causal claim. Each requires the coherence of cause-and-effect.

Gaudapāda has shown — in the arguments reviewed in Section 9.5 — that cause-and-effect is incoherent as a category. If the relationship between cause and effect cannot be established (because the sequencing of prior and posterior cannot be determined without circularity), then the entire story of past-event-producing-impression-being-carried-forward-to-present collapses. There is no coherent account of how a past event deposits a present memory. The mechanism by which vāsanā accumulates and projects is shown to be, at the level of ultimate analysis, logically empty.

The three-state analysis contributes a further blow. Memory, Gaudapāda observes, is a function that belongs to the waking and dream states. In deep sleep — suṣupti — memory ceases entirely. As Ānandagiri's commentary notes plainly: 'Even memory does not function then.' If memory were a fundamental feature of Consciousness — if the causal body's impressions were genuinely stored at the deepest level of being — they would be present in suṣupti. But they are not. During deep sleep, no specific content-memory functions — no particular impression presents itself as named recall, no personal history is rehearsed. What wakes is not a self that has retrieved its stored vāsanās from a causal-body archive, but a self-imagination that reconstitutes its sense of history from the same generative loop Kārikā 2.16 describes. The impressions are not retrieved; they are regenerated. The impressions that supposedly accumulated across a lifetime vanish during every night of dreamless sleep, only to reconstitute themselves upon waking — not because they were retrieved from storage, but because the Jīva-imagination restarts its self-generating loop.

The Kārikā also notes — in its analysis of the waking-dream relationship (Kārikā 4.37) — that the waking state is said to be the cause of dream because dream borrows its imagery from waking impressions. But if dream objects are unreal, then the waking impressions that supposedly caused them are as unreal as the effects they produced. The causal chain running from waking vāsanā to dream appearance is itself dream-like: part of the appearance, not the explanation of it.

This is the precise logical point at which the vivartavāda account of vāsanā is shown to have been provisional. The causal body was never a real storehouse. Vāsanās were never real accumulated impressions. They were, themselves, part of the superimposition — the superimposition of the idea of a personal history, a carried-forward identity, a world with causal texture, upon the simplicity of Awareness that has no history, carries nothing forward, and requires no mechanism to be what it is.

#### 2.2.3. **The Phenomenological Complement: Memory as a Present-Moment Thought**

Gaudapāda's logical argument removes the theoretical ground from under memory's claims. What Greg Goode's phenomenological method adds — and this is its precise contribution, not substituting for Gaudapāda but completing him in a different register — is the direct experiential investigation of what memory actually is when examined as it appears, right now, in this moment.

The investigation begins with a simple but unsettling observation: you never actually experience the past.

What you call 'remembering' is always and only a thought appearing now. When you 'remember' yesterday's conversation, there is no yesterday's conversation presenting itself to you. There is a thought — arising in the present moment, in present-moment Awareness — that carries the label 'yesterday's conversation.' The labelling is itself a further present-moment operation: a thought about the thought, naming it as 'memory' rather than 'imagination' or 'fantasy.' The distinction between memory and fantasy is itself made by a present-moment judgment — a thought that evaluates another thought as corresponding to a real past event. But this evaluating thought is present. The past event it claims to correspond to is not present. It is, precisely, absent. What is present is the thought of it.

Follow this carefully, because it is subtle. The claim is not that your memories are false — that yesterday's conversation did not happen, that your personal history is fictional. The claim is more precise and more radical: that whatever did or did not happen in the past, what you have access to right now is only a present thought labelled 'past.' The past itself is never experienced. Only a present thought about the past is experienced. And a present thought labelled 'past' is still a present thought.

The same analysis applies, with equal precision, to anticipation. What you call 'thinking about tomorrow' is a thought appearing now, labelled 'future.' The future is never experienced — only a present thought carrying the attribution 'this refers to something not yet present.' Strip the label away, and what remains is simply a thought arising in present-moment Awareness, like any other thought.

Past and future are not dimensions of time in which events actually reside. They are attributions — labels applied by a present-moment thought to other present-moment thoughts, creating the appearance of a temporal dimension that extends in both directions from a located 'now.' The 'now' itself, when examined, turns out not to be a point in a temporal sequence but the only mode of experience that ever exists. Nothing is ever experienced in the past. Nothing is ever experienced in the future. Experience is always and only now.

_[PAUSE AND INVESTIGATE]Notice, right now, that you are reading these words. This reading is happening now. Not in the past, not in the future — now. Now bring to mind a recent memory — something from this morning, or yesterday. Notice that what actually arises is a thought. A present-moment thought. The event itself is not here. The thought about it is here. Now notice the labelling: something in the present moment evaluates that thought and calls it 'memory' — assigns it to 'the past.' Watch the labelling happening. It is happening now. Ask: where is the past? Not in concept, but actually — where does it reside? Can you find the past as anything other than a present thought carrying the label 'past'? Do not answer quickly. Look._

When this investigation is conducted honestly, something vertiginous becomes visible. The entire structure of personal identity — the sense of being a person with a history, shaped by past events, moving toward a future — is a structure built entirely from present-moment thoughts labelled as references to a past and a future that are never directly experienced. The causal body with its accumulated vāsanās, the personal history that stretches behind you, the karma that shapes your present — all of these rest on the assumption that there is a real past in which real impressions were deposited by real events. When you look for that past directly, you find only present thoughts. The past is always a thought now. The vāsanā is always a thought now. The accumulated weight of history is always a thought now.

#### 2.2.4. **The Two Deconstructions, One Conclusion**

Gaudapāda's logical argument and Goode's phenomenological investigation arrive at the same recognition from different directions, and their convergence is precisely what gives the recognition its force.

Gaudapāda shows: the causal mechanism by which vāsanās supposedly accumulate and project the world is logically incoherent. Causation cannot establish the relationship between a past event and its present residue, because the prior-and-posterior ordering that causation requires cannot be coherently established. Memory-as-mechanism is empty.

Goode shows: what memory actually is, when examined phenomenologically rather than as a theoretical mechanism, is a present thought labelled 'past.' There is no experiential access to the past — only present-moment Awareness knowing a present-moment thought. Memory-as-experience is always now.

Together, they close every possible avenue by which memory could function as the bridge between a real past and a real present. The bridge has no logical structure (Gaudapāda) and no experiential reality (Goode). What appears to be the weight of accumulated history — the vāsanās driving the compulsive re-experiencing of the same patterns, the seeming inevitability of a personal story — is seen to be something that exists only as a thought arising now, a thought that the next thought will call 'memory' and attribute to a past that is itself only another thought.

This does not mean personal history is irrelevant, or that the body's conditioning does not shape experience. At the conventional level, all of this operates and continues to operate. The person continues to function, the body continues to carry its tendencies, patterns continue to appear. The deconstruction of memory as a real causal mechanism does not make the patterns invisible. It makes them ungrounded. They no longer rest on the bedrock of a real accumulated past that must be worked through, managed, transcended. They arise as thoughts — vivid, sometimes compelling, sometimes painful — and they pass. Without a real past to have caused them, without a real future they must inevitably produce, they are simply what they are: appearances in Awareness, substanceless as the circles drawn by the whirling firebrand.

The firebrand's circles, to which we now turn, are the perfect illustration of exactly this: an appearance that has no causal history, that was never deposited as an impression from a previous configuration of the brand, that arises entirely in the now of the brand's movement, and vanishes entirely when the movement ceases — without going anywhere, without leaving a residue, without depositing an impression in the fire-brand that will require future burning.

There are no vāsanās in the fire-brand. There is only the fire-brand, burning, as it always was.



### 2.3. The Firebrand: Experience Without Production

Gaudapāda is acutely aware that this conclusion creates a problem that is not merely philosophical but phenomenological. If nothing has been created, what is this appearance I am having right now? I see a world. I experience a body. I have thoughts. These are not nothing. If ajātivāda is true, these appearances need explaining — or rather, their appearing-without-being-produced needs illustration, because 'explaining' is already too causal a word.

The illustration Gaudapāda deploys for this purpose is one of the most celebrated in the Sanskrit philosophical tradition: the alaṭa, the firebrand. Take a burning stick and whirl it in darkness. You see circles of fire. Straight lines. Arcs. Patterns. They appear vivid, continuous, seemingly real. Yet examine what has actually occurred:

**Kārikā 4.47–4.48**

*ṛjuvakrādikābhāsamalātaspanditaṃ yathā | grahaṇagrāhakābhāsaṃ vijñānaspanditaṃ tathā || aspandamānamalātamanābhāsamajaṃ yathā | aspandamānaṃ vijñānamanābhāsamajaṃ tathā ||*

*As a fire-brand, when set in motion, appears as straight, crooked, and so on, so also Consciousness, when set in motion, appears as the perceiver, the perceived, and the like. As the fire-brand, when not in motion, is free from all appearances and remains changeless, similarly, Consciousness, when not in motion through ignorance, is free from all appearances and remains changeless and unborn.*

**Kārikā 4.49–4.50**

*alāte spandamāne vai nā''bhāsā anyatobhuvaḥ | na tato'nyatra nispandānnālātaṃ praviśanti te || na nirgatā alātātte dravyatvābhāvayogataḥ | vijñāne'pi tathaiva syurābhāsasyāviśeṣataḥ ||*

*When the fire-brand is in motion, the appearances that are seen in it do not come from elsewhere. When the fire-brand is not moved, the appearances do not go elsewhere. Further, the appearances do not enter into the fire-brand when it is motionless. The appearances do not emerge from the fire-brand as something that comes out of a house — because appearances are not of the nature of substance. This also applies to Consciousness on account of the similarity of appearances in both cases.*

**Kārikā 4.51–4.52**

*vijñāne spandamāne vai nā''bhāsā anyatobhuvaḥ | na tato'nyatra nispandānna vijñānaṃ viśanti te || na nirgatāste vijñānāddravyatvābhāvayogataḥ | kāryakāraṇatābhāvādyato'cintyāḥ sadaiva te ||*

*When Consciousness is associated with the idea of activity — as in dream and waking states — the appearances that are seen in it do not come from elsewhere. When Consciousness is inactive — as in deep sleep — appearances do not go elsewhere. Further, appearances do not enter into it. The appearances have not emerged from Consciousness because they are not of the nature of substance. Since they are without causal relationship, they are eternally beyond thought.*

The firebrand analogy illuminates three things simultaneously, and all three are essential.

First: the appearances are not imported from outside. The circles of fire do not arrive from some other location when the stick is moved. There is no external source from which objects are drawn into Consciousness to be experienced. This rules out dualist theories in which the world exists independently of Consciousness and impresses itself upon it.

Second: the appearances do not depart to some other location when the stick is still. The circles do not 'go somewhere' when the stick stops moving. Objects do not have a storage location outside Consciousness to which they retreat when not being experienced. This rules out theories in which objects persist in some mind-independent realm.

Third: the appearances are not produced as substances. They have no independent substantiality. A circle of fire has no mass, no location, no duration as an entity. It is an appearance — a visual event that cannot be located as a thing either inside or outside the fire-brand. It neither enters the fire-brand when the stick stills nor exits it when the stick moves.

The application to Consciousness is exact. What appears in Consciousness — the world, the body, thoughts, perceptions — does not arrive from outside Consciousness, does not depart to some mind-independent location when experience ceases, and is not produced as a substance with independent ontological standing. It is appearance without substantiality, experience without production.

The key philosophical move, which distinguishes ajātivāda from vivartavāda, is contained in the phrase: the appearances are not of the nature of substance. Vivartavāda taught that the world is an illusory superimposition — like silver superimposed upon mother-of-pearl. The silver appears; the mother-of-pearl is the reality. The appearance is granted some degree of ontological standing: it appears; it is 'there' in some sense, however illusorily. Ajātivāda retracts even this. The circle of fire is not an illusory substance superimposed upon a real substance. It is not a substance at all — illusory or otherwise. It is an appearance that has no ontological weight, no causal history, no location. It arises without being produced and ceases without going anywhere. It is, in the precise Kārikā term, acintya: beyond thought, because the categories thought employs — substance, production, causation, location — do not apply to it.

This is not a mystical claim. It is a rigorous consequence of the preceding argument. If causation is incoherent, then nothing is ever produced as a caused substance. If nothing is ever produced as a caused substance, the appearances that seem to arise and subside are not productions — they are appearances without ontological weight, like the fire-circle, vivid but substanceless.

### 2.4.  Manana: The Five Objections

Ajātivāda generates sharper and more destabilizing objections than any preceding stage of the inquiry, precisely because it is more radical. These objections deserve philosophical engagement, not spiritual sidestepping.

***Objection 1: If nothing was created, even illusorily, what is this appearance I am having right now?***

This is the most immediate and psychologically urgent objection, and it is crucial not to trivialize it. The experience of a world, a body, thoughts, sensations — this is undeniably present. Ajātivāda does not deny that experience occurs. What it denies is that experience involves the production of substantial objects. The fire-circle is vivid. No one claims the burning stick does not appear to make circles when whirled. The denial is not of appearance but of the ontological weight the appearance seems to carry: the apparent circle was never produced as a circle-substance with causal history and independent location. Similarly, the world of experience is vivid and undeniable as experience. What ajātivāda denies is that the world was ever produced as a world-substance, a collection of independently existing entities with causal relationships between them. Appearances can be vivid without being substances. This is precisely what the firebrand demonstrates.

***Objection 2: But vivartavāda already taught that the world is an illusory appearance in Awareness. Doesn't ajātivāda just repeat this?***

This is perhaps the subtlest objection, and getting it right is what distinguishes genuine philosophical progress from the mere multiplication of synonyms. Vivartavāda teaches that the world is an illusory appearance — vivarta — superimposed on Brahman, as silver is superimposed on mother-of-pearl. The superimposition has ontological standing as an appearance: it appears; it is mithyā (false) but not non-existent; it is something that has shown up, even if it has no ultimate reality. The Witness, in vivartavāda, still has objects to witness — illusory ones, but objects. The observer-observed structure is preserved, even if the observed side has been demoted from 'real' to 'illusory.'

Ajātivāda retracts the concession that an illusory appearance was produced. The fire-circle was never produced — not illusorily, not apparently, not in any sense. What appears has no more ontological weight than the circle: it is appearance without substance, experience without production. The difference between vivartavāda and ajātivāda is therefore not a difference in degree — 'even more unreal' — but a difference in the logical structure of what is being claimed. Vivartavāda: the world appears, but what appears has no ultimate substance. Ajātivāda: nothing was produced, even as an appearance with the ontological standing of being-produced-as-appearance. The circle does not arise as a produced circle-appearance. It simply seems to be there while the stick is moving, and the seeming itself has no causal history.

***Objection 3: If I was never bound, what was the point of all the previous chapters of this book?***

Gaudapāda addresses this with precise equanimity in verse 2.32:

**Kārikā 2.32**

*na nirodho na cotpattirna baddho na ca sādhakaḥ | na mumukṣurna vai mukta ityeṣā paramārthatā ||*

*There is no dissolution, no birth, none in bondage, no one adopting means for attainment, no seeker after liberation, and no one liberated. This is the Ultimate Truth.*

From the standpoint of paramārtha — ultimate reality — this verse is literally and completely true. There was never a bound person, never a seeker, never a practice, never a liberation. But this verse is not the first verse of the book. It is available only to a mind that has already traversed the preceding path — that has worked through the body-mind identification, the gross suffering, the seeking-mind's many strategies, the vivartavāda recognition, and the vāsanā-burning of the DDV. The statement that no liberation occurred is comprehensible as truth only to the one who no longer needs liberation. Offered to the one who still feels bound, it functions not as liberation but as spiritual bypassing: the use of an absolute-level statement to dismiss conventional-level reality. The path was not a detour. It was the very process by which the mind that could hear 'there was no path' was prepared to hear it.

***Objection 4: Does ajātivāda collapse into Mādhyamaka Śūnyatā — the Buddhist teaching that everything is empty?***

Gaudapāda was clearly aware of the Mādhyamaka school and engages its positions at several points in the Kārikā. The distinction, however, is precise. Mādhyamaka śūnyatā — emptiness — is a denial of own-nature (svabhāva): things lack inherent existence, are empty of self-subsistent being. This is a doctrine about the things that appear. Ajātivāda is not a doctrine about the things that appear. It is a doctrine about the ground: Brahman, pure Consciousness, is unborn, undivided, self-luminous. The denial of production is not a denial of Consciousness — it is an assertion of what Consciousness is, once the false attribution of production to it has been removed. Where Mādhyamaka śūnyatā risks the charge of nihilism (what is left, if everything is empty?), ajātivāda has a clear answer: Brahman remains, as it always was. Not as a creator who did not create. As the sole reality that was never not-sole.

***Objection 5: If the Witness dissolves, what is left? Does the sense of 'I' dissolve entirely?***

This is the question that makes the final section of this chapter necessary, and it is addressed there in full.

## 3. Nididhyāsana: The End of the Witness

### 3.1. When the Object Disappears, the Witness Has Nowhere to Stand

Return now to what the Witness actually is.

The Witness is Awareness insofar as Awareness appears to stand apart from its objects — observing them, knowing them, remaining unchanged while they change. This 'standing apart' is not a metaphysical fact about two separate substances. It is a phenomenological description: the experience, arising from discriminative inquiry, of being the knowing presence rather than the changing content. Awareness appears as Witness when the mind, trained by the DDV's discrimination, has ceased to merge automatically with its objects and recognizes itself as what knows them.

This recognition is real. It is not wrong. Chapter 8 was not in error. But it rested, without the DDV's own resources to reveal this, on an assumption: that there are objects there to be witnessed. Not objects with independent substance — the DDV had already dissolved that. But objects in the sense of produced appearances: things that showed up, events that occurred, experiences that arose as produced experiential content. Vivartavāda granted this much: the world is an illusory appearance, but it appears. And the Witness has this appearance as its object.

Ajātivāda withdraws that assumption. If causation is incoherent, no production has occurred. If no production has occurred, the objects — even as illusory appearances — were never produced as objects. There is no produced world for the Witness to witness.

The Witness position requires an object. A Witness that witnesses nothing is not a Witness — it is just Awareness. The observer-observed structure is definitionally dualistic: it requires two sides. Remove the produced-object side, and the observer side has nothing to stand against. The structure collapses — not into void, but back into what it always was: Awareness, undivided, the sole reality.

This is what the tradition means when it says the Witness dissolves. Not that Awareness ceases. Not that the sense of presence disappears. Not that experience goes dark. Awareness remains — but it is no longer Awareness-in-the-role-of-Witness, positioned over against produced objects. It is simply Awareness, as it was before the apparent position of Witness was adopted. The circle of fire has stilled. The fire-brand remains, exactly what it always was: a single point of light.

### 3.2.  Sahaja: The Natural State

What the Kārikā points toward, in its final verses on the nature of Ātman, is not a new state to be achieved but the recognition of what was never not the case:

**Kārikā 2.32 and 4.92**

*There is no dissolution, no birth, none in bondage, no seeker after liberation, none liberated. This is the Ultimate Truth. The mind, which is pure Consciousness, is not born and does not die. The whole world of appearance is due to the seeming motion of Consciousness, which is in reality motionless. When the seeming motion ceases, the nature of pure unborn Consciousness is realized.*

The word the tradition uses for this recognition is sahaja — the natural state. Sahaja means 'born together with,' suggesting something innate, original, not achieved. It is the standing of Awareness in its own nature, not in the role of Witness positioned over against objects, not in identification with the body-mind, but simply as what it is: unborn, undivided, the sole reality it has always been.

Sahaja is not a state you enter. It is the cessation of what made it seem you were ever not in it. The Witness stage involved, by structural necessity, a subtle holding: the observer position required maintenance. That maintenance was so subtle — so much more refined than the gross effort of ordinary concentration — that it registered as natural. It becomes apparent only in retrospect, from within sahaja, as having been an imperceptible contraction. When ajātivāda is recognized not as philosophical position but as direct seeing — when the logical conclusion that no production has occurred is not merely understood but seen — that subtle holding is released. Not because effort has been applied to release it. Because the ground for the observer position is seen to have been absent.

This is the difference between Stage Three and Stage Four that cannot be captured in a comparison of descriptions, because the difference is not in what is described but in the structure of the describer. At Stage Three, Awareness is described as witnessing objects. At Stage Four, there is only Awareness — the distinction between witnessing and what is witnessed has no ground to stand on.

### 3.3. The Return to the Beginning

This book began with a teenager sitting on his engineering college grounds, watching the algorithm of worldly happiness unravel. Get into 10th grade, then engineering college, then MBA, then the high-profile job, then — death. Every external reward revealed a new lack. Every satisfaction contained the seed of new craving. The sense of being a separate, incomplete, vulnerable person who must acquire and achieve in order finally, somehow, to become whole.

That predicament was real. The suffering was real. The urgency of the question 'What am I?' — arising from the recognition that the strategies of the separate self never resolve the underlying condition — was real.

Ajātivāda's answer to that predicament is the most radical answer possible. It does not say: the separate self can be improved, refined, enlightened. It does not say: the separate self is real but can learn to merge with something larger. It says: the separate self was never produced. The person who felt the suffering, ran the strategies, undertook the inquiry, stabilized the Witness, and now reads this sentence — was never produced as a separate entity. What was there all along was Brahman, appearing to be a separate person through the movement of Avidyā — ignorance — in the way a fire-brand appears to create circles of fire through the movement of the one who holds it.

This is not nihilism, because it does not deny that something is here. Something is undeniably here. Awareness is present. Experience occurs. The body functions. Relationships exist. What is denied is that these phenomena involve separately produced entities with independent ontological standing. The circle of fire is vivid. The fire-brand, the one who holds it, the darkness — all real. The circle itself: appearance without substance, vividness without production.

When this is seen — not as a philosophical position to adopt but as a direct recognition — the predicament with which this book opened is not solved. It is dissolved. The separate self that had the problem was never produced. The person who needed completion was never produced as a person who lacked anything. Brahman alone was here, is here, will be here: unborn, undivided, complete — the sole reality appearing, through the movement of seeming, as everything that ever seemed to be other than itself.

The inquiry is complete. Not because a final answer has been found and secured, but because the questioner who needed a final answer has been seen through. What remains is not the absence of a person. It is the presence of what was always already here, before the person seemed to begin: the unborn, the undivided, the one reality that the twelve terse verses of the Māṇḍūkya Upaniṣad point to in their final, irreducible statement — Turīya. The fourth. The Self. This should be known.

## 4. Summary

This chapter has unfolded Gaudapāda's ajātivāda — the doctrine of non-origination — as the philosophical culmination of the inquiry begun in Chapter 1\.

The first movement established the text and its central claim: the Māṇḍūkya Upaniṣad points to Turīya, the unborn Self; Gaudapāda's Kārikā develops this into a systematic philosophical argument. The survey of creation theories — from God's will to Time to sport to enjoyment — showed that each theory attributes to Brahman a condition incompatible with its nature. The mutual refutations of Sāṃkhya and Nyāya-Vaiśeṣika — existence from existing, existence from non-existing — inadvertently demonstrate the incoherence of causation itself, which Gaudapāda accepts as confirmation that non-origination is the only non-contradictory position. The provisional teaching of creation theories, including vivartavāda, was shown to be compassionate pedagogy for minds not yet prepared for the full negation — not error, but stepping stone.

The second movement conducted the logical deconstruction of cause and effect: the identical-cause argument, the different-cause argument, the immortal-mortal impossibility, and the sequencing incoherence — each exposing a different fault in the concept of production. The firebrand analogy illustrated how appearances can be vivid without being produced as substances: the circle of fire neither arrives from outside the brand, nor departs to some external location, nor enters the brand when it stills — it is appearance without ontological weight. The five objections to ajātivāda were addressed: appearance is not denied, only its substantiality; the distinction between vivartavāda and ajātivāda was clarified as a difference in logical structure, not degree; the path was shown to be necessary preparation even for the recognition that no path was needed; the difference from Mādhyamaka śūnyatā was established; and the dissolution of the Witness was identified as the subject of the third movement.

The third movement drew the philosophical thread to its conclusion. The Witness requires an object. Ajātivāda, by showing that no production has occurred, removes the produced-object side of the observer-observed structure. Without an object, the Witness position has no ground. It dissolves — not into void but back into what was always there: Awareness in its natural state (sahaja), unborn, undivided, the sole reality. The book's opening predicament was shown to dissolve with it: the separate self who had the problem was never produced. What remains is Brahman alone — as it always was.
