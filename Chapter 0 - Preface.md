My journey in self-inquiry started in college when I was about twenty years old. Like young Nachiketa in the Katha Upanishad, I was suddenly gripped by a terrible clarity: life was meaningless and purposeless in the face of death. Every worldly achievement—every prize, every triumph, every carefully constructed identity—would ultimately be claimed by death, dissolved back into nothing. Nachiketa at least had intimations of something beyond, some reality that death could not touch. I had no such comfort. Born in India, the very soil that had given birth to Nachiketa’s fierce questioning millennia ago, I had somehow inherited none of that wisdom. My education, like that of so many middle-class Indians of my generation, had been thoroughly secular, rooted in science and material success. Spiritual inquiry belonged to temple rituals and grandmother’s stories, not to serious young men planning careers. I set out on what would become a long road of self-inquiry without any map, without any framework for understanding something beyond the material world, without even knowing if such a thing existed.

My first serious book on spirituality was one from my father’s collection—_The Autobiography of a Yogi_ by Paramahansa Yogananda, steeped in mysticism and filled with accounts of yogic miracles. This book had such a powerful impact on my young, vulnerable mind that I came within a hair’s breadth of donning the saffron robe and becoming a renunciant. Then destiny intervened. One day, strolling through my college library, I watched as a book inexplicably tipped from a shelf and fell at my feet. On its cover was a man with a magnetic face. The book was _The Network of Thought_ by Jiddu Krishnamurti.

To my scientific, rationally oriented mind, this book’s appeal was instant. I received it as parched earth receives rain. Krishnamurti would remain the mainstay of my inquiry for the next seventeen years, though I would periodically revolt against his teachings, struggling to reconcile what he pointed toward with my need for some method, some way forward. What drew me to him was his relentless questioning of every belief system. He showed me early on that God is nothing but a conception created by mind to give itself solace. He taught me the crucial Advaitic principle of negation—that truth cannot be found positively, through adding beliefs or practices, but only through the negation of falsity. Most importantly, he showed me that true meditation is not about posture, formal sitting practice, or technique—these are all positive approaches. Instead, he demonstrated what he called “choiceless awareness of what is”—a negative approach, a pure seeing without the interference of the chooser. I later learned that this is the exact principle found in Advaita, most explicitly in the text _Drig-Drishya Viveka_/DDV (The Discrimination of the Seer and the Seen). The difference is that Krishnamurti approaches this psychologically—examining the movement of thought and the structure of the self—whereas DDV approaches it phenomenologically—investigating the nature of experience itself and what Witnesses it. While Krishnamurti stopped at the dissolution of psychological conditioning, texts like _Drig-Drishya Viveka_ take the final step by revealing the Witness, the ever-present reality. His “choiceless awareness” clears the ground; Advaita points to the ground itself as one’s own nature.

My entire approach to self-inquiry was thus never about meditation practice or spiritual technique, but rather this subtle practice of choiceless awareness that could be carried out in the midst of daily life. When pure negation proved too steep—when I needed some positive framework to work with—I found help in the writings of Ken Wilber, who employs the skillful teaching method of alternating between superimposition (providing positive frameworks) and negation (deconstructing them), a method traditional to Advaita.

Yet Krishnamurti had taken me only to the doorstep of truth. Through him I could see the limitations of mind clearly. Through pure logic and negation, I had come to understand that there must be a formless reality beyond time and space, and that this reality must be what I fundamentally am. But I had no idea how to access it, how to shift from conceptual understanding to direct recognition. Krishnamurti’s teaching offered nothing for this final step. Worse, he framed ultimate realization in terms of a mystical experience. This, I would later realize, is one of the most dangerous traps for seekers of non-duality, because it turns recognition into an attainment, some extraordinary experience in time that must happen to you, rather than the seeing of what already is.

Then, once again, destiny intervened. I happened to be casually reading a book by Swami Vivekananda when I came across the line: “You are God.” I didn’t concern myself with the word “God” because Vivekananda wasn’t using it in the conventional sense of a personal creator. He was pointing toward Advaita. Astonishingly, after nearly two decades of inquiry, this was the first time I had encountered the word Advaita. I dove in immediately.

I started with a slim introductory text, which I understood almost instantaneously—Krishnamurti had already grounded me deeply in the Advaitic approach without my knowing it. I just needed the tipping point, the final clarification. That came through the writings of James Swartz, whose articles I discovered online. Swartz continuously and emphatically hammered home the central Advaitic insight: liberation (_moksha_) is not about peak experiences, mystical states, or any kind of special occurrence. It is about direct knowledge (_aparokshanubhuti_)—immediate recognition that is eternal and independent of all experiences. This was the missing piece.

On December 4, 2012, while reading one of Swartz’s articles on the Advaitic insight called _akhandakara vritti_ (the continuous, unbroken thought-wave of “I am Brahman”), something shifted. As soon as I finished the article, in the most dramatic fashion, I had my own _akhandakara vritti_. I recognized myself as _sakshi_—the Witness, the unborn, eternal Self/Awareness/Consciousness. This is what Advaita calls “direct knowledge,” a recognition that doesn’t come and go with changing experiences but remains constant regardless of what appears. The existential terror that had haunted me since college dissolved. I was no longer the limited, dying person I had taken myself to be. I was the infinite, eternal Awareness within which that person appeared.

Most Advaita presentations emphasize this recognition of the Witness and consider it sufficient. But through conversations with Buddhist friends online, I came to understand that a subtle duality remained—between the Witness and what is Witnessed, between Awareness and its contents. This bothered me. If reality is truly non-dual, how could there be two things—a Witness and what is Witnessed? I searched for Advaita teachers who could help me dissolve this final duality. I found none who addressed it in a truly comprehensive manner.

Then I discovered Greg Goode’s book on the Direct Path. In its final section, he described the deconstruction of the Witness. I read it again and again, but couldn’t grasp what he was pointing toward. The confusion arose because the Direct Path (based on the teachings of Atmananda Krishna Menon and transmitted through teachers like Francis Lucille and Greg Goode) has a different pedagogy for the “Witness” than traditional Advaita does. I had numerous conversations with Greg about this, even offering at one point to abandon Advaita and study Madhyamika Buddhism with him instead—he is also a teacher of that tradition. He graciously offered to teach me, but somehow I kept returning to the Advaita framework. It was what I knew, what had worked for me.

Finally, Greg pointed me toward the _Mandukya Upanishad_ and Gaudapada’s _Karika_ (commentary) on it. When I read Gaudapada’s exposition of _Ajativada_—the ultimate teaching of Advaita which shows that there is no creation, no origination, that ultimately even the illusory appearance of multiplicity never arose—everything clicked into place. Seven years after recognizing myself as Witness, Gaudapada’s words dissolved the duality between Witness and Witnessed. There was no separate Witness standing apart from experience, observing it. There was only non-dual Consciousness/Awareness, with no separation between the Witness and the witnessed, knower and known, seer and seen. 

This recognition has continued to deepen and integrate in the years since. The understanding itself is complete—what else is there to recognize once you’ve seen that everything is Consciousness? There’s nothing more fundamental than non-dual Awareness, nothing beyond it to attain. What continues to unfold is not understanding but integration—how this recognition expresses itself in daily life, relationships, and teaching. But what became clear through this journey—from existential terror through Krishnamurti’s negation, through Swartz’s emphasis on direct knowledge, through the Witness stage, to Gaudapada’s uncompromising non-dualism—was this: the recognition I was seeking required no belief in God, no religious framework, no theological concepts. What I am is simply what is—Consciousness itself, appearing as this entire display we call reality. This is what I want to share in this book.

This book arises from a compelling need to show that Advaita can be pursued in a wholly secular way—free from cultural trappings and belief in a creator God—while remaining fully rooted in its own classical tradition. I also wanted to show that this is not some new-fangled approach but something already present in the rich tradition of Advaita, though less emphasized in contemporary presentations. My recognition of what I am came through studying the writings of James Swartz, a teacher of traditional Vedanta. His clear explanations and systematic presentation of Advaita dissolved my confusion and allowed direct seeing to occur. I owe him an enormous debt, and this book exists because of what his teaching revealed.

However, readers will notice that the approach I present here differs significantly from many presentations of traditional Vedanta in not requiring theistic concepts. This isn’t a rejection of what James Swartz teaches—his presentation works powerfully for many people, and it worked for me in precipitating recognition. Rather, it’s my attempt to distill what I found absolutely essential from cultural and religious overlay.

When *aparokshanubhuti* (direct recognition) occurred through his teaching, what became clear was simply this: I am not the limited body-mind I had taken myself to be, but rather the infinite Awareness appearing as all experience. This recognition didn’t require me to believe in Ishvara (God as cosmic creator), didn’t depend on accepting karma theory or reincarnation, and didn’t necessitate any theological framework. The recognition stood on its own.

This book attempts to present the means to that core recognition as clearly as possible without the traditional religious and cultural elements. I don’t claim my approach is superior to traditional presentations incorporating God as a concept—many people find the fuller Vedantic framework helpful and meaningful. But for contemporary seekers—particularly those with scientific backgrounds, those wounded by religious institutions, or those who simply cannot accept theological claims they cannot verify—this approach removes unnecessary barriers to what Advaita actually points toward.

Moreover, both Shankara and Gaudapada—the most authoritative voices in the Advaita tradition—clearly indicate that Ishvara (God as creator) is a provisional teaching (vyavaharika) useful for preliminary understanding but ultimately transcended in the highest knowledge (paramarthika). So I’m not departing from tradition but highlighting what classical Advaita itself teaches: the recognition of non-dual Awareness stands complete without requiring belief in a creator God.

For the past five years, I’ve taught Advaita through NEEV Trust, a non-profit organization founded by me and dedicated to sharing the direct path of self-inquiry. Working closely with seekers has taught me as much as my own inquiry did. Teaching forces precision. Students ask questions you never thought to ask yourself. They get stuck in places you sailed past without noticing. They need the teaching presented from angles you hadn’t considered. This process has clarified my own understanding immeasurably and shown me exactly how and for whom this approach works.

The Advaita tradition speaks honestly about prerequisites. When one climbs a mountain, the most direct path is also the steepest. This book presents Jnana Yoga—the path of knowledge through direct inquiry—without the preparatory stages traditionally considered necessary. This means it works best for certain people.

**This book is for you if:**

- You’re drawn to philosophical investigation and can follow sustained conceptual analysis
- You have basic emotional stability and aren’t in acute psychological crisis
- You’re willing to question your most fundamental viewpoints and beliefs
- You can tolerate uncertainty and the temporary destabilization that inquiry can bring
- You’re intellectually honest enough to look directly at what’s true rather than what’s comforting
- You’re skeptical of or uninterested in devotional approaches or belief in God

**This book may not be for you if:**

- You’re dealing with severe depression, trauma, or psychological instability (address that first with appropriate professional support)
- You’re naturally drawn to devotional practice and find the bhakti path meaningful (other teachers work with that approach)
- You prefer gradual, systematic methods with clear stages and measurable progress
- You need immediate practical techniques for managing anxiety or stress (this addresses the deeper existential questions, not psychological symptoms)
- You’re looking for a philosophy to believe in rather than a method to investigate what you are

I don’t say this to gatekeep or create hierarchy. The other yogic paths—karma yoga (selfless action), bhakti yoga (devotion), raja yoga (systematic meditation practice)—work as preparatory practices for those suited to them. But if you’re reading this and recognize yourself in the first list rather than the second, this book offers something you may not find elsewhere: a complete path grounded in traditional Advaita to recognizing your nature without requiring religious belief, devotional practice, or years of preparatory discipline.

**What to Expect from This Book**

This book will not offer comfort, reassurance, or ready-made conclusions. It will not promise bliss, permanent peace as an experience, or an improved version of your personality. What it offers instead is liberating clarity—through sustained inquiry into what you actually are. It points toward the peace that was always present as your nature, untouched by the presence or absence of any experience. You can expect careful conceptual analysis paired with direct investigations into your own experience. At times the inquiry may feel destabilizing, as long-held assumptions about self, meaning, and purpose are questioned without replacement. This is not a book to be consumed passively. It asks something of you: honesty, patience, and the willingness to remain with not-knowing until seeing occurs on its own.

**Acknowledgment of Limitations**

A brief note about what I am and am not qualified to address: I’m not a psychologist, neuroscientist, or licensed mental health professional. When this book touches on psychological concepts—trauma, emotional regulation, mental health—I do so as an informed layperson drawing on current research and clinical understanding, not as an expert in those fields. Where psychological support is needed, I encourage you to seek qualified professionals. What I can speak to with confidence is the Advaita tradition and the practice of self-inquiry, both from my own direct recognition and from years of teaching others. This book stays within those boundaries.
