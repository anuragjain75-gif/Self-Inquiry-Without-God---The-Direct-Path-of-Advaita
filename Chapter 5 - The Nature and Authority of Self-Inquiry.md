## **Introduction**

Chapter 4 cleared the ground. Materialism, consumerism, progress ideology, and individualism—those invisible frameworks through which we filter every question about ourselves—were examined as lived structures, not abstract philosophical positions. Through sustained psycho-philosophical inquiry, they loosened their grip enough that the fundamental question could finally be held without automatic answers rushing in.

Now the question stands: What am I? Not rhetorical, not philosophical speculation—urgent existential inquiry. The question remains open.

This chapter presents the method for investigating that question. Not as a belief system to accept, but as a direct investigation you can begin right now. Self-inquiry is Advaita's core practice, the means through which what previous chapters explained conceptually becomes direct recognition.

We proceed in four movements: first establishing what self-inquiry actually is, and distinguishing it sharply from what it resembles but is not; then tracing its roots in the two classical texts that form this book's methodological foundation; then examining a question that every serious seeker eventually asks—why appeal to scriptural authority at all if you can verify everything in direct experience?; and finally resolving the tension that gives this book its specific character, namely how self-inquiry can draw on Advaitic scripture while requiring no theistic belief whatsoever.

## **1\. What Is Self-Inquiry According to Advaita?**

Self-inquiry is not thinking about yourself. This distinction matters fundamentally but gets obscured by language itself. The phrase self-inquiry seems to name a mental activity—something the mind does, an examination of the self as object. But authentic atma-vicara is the opposite of this.

When you think about yourself—analyzing personality traits, examining motivations, constructing narratives about who you are—you are engaged in psychological introspection. This can be valuable therapeutically. It is what therapy cultivates, what journaling explores. All of this involves thoughts about the self: mental content, psychological analysis, conceptual understanding.

Self-inquiry investigates what is aware of all this mental content, not the content itself. When the thought I am insecure appears, psychological introspection asks: why am I insecure? Self-inquiry asks a prior question: what is aware of the thought 'I am insecure'? What knows that insecurity is present?

This shift—from content to context, from observed to observer, from what appears to what is aware of the appearance—is what self-inquiry means. Not analyzing the self but investigating the investigator. Not improving the person but questioning whether the person you take yourself to be actually exists as you imagine.

### **1.1 The Classical Method: Neti-Neti**

Self-inquiry operates through the classical Advaitic method of neti-neti—not this, not this—a disciplined negation of everything that can be known as an object. The reason is both philosophical and verifiable in direct experience: whatever is known stands as object, and every object is finite, changing, and dependent on the knower.

The Upanisads insist that ultimate reality cannot be grasped as an object of knowledge. The Brhadaranyaka Upanisad (2.3.6) states this with uncompromising clarity: neti neti—the Self is reached not by defining it, but by negating all that is not the Self. This is not a movement toward emptiness but toward the one reality that cannot be objectified because it is the very light by which all objects are known.

The Kena Upanisad (1.5) expresses this with precision philosophy has rarely matched: That which the mind cannot think, but by which the mind thinks—know That to be Brahman. The verse is deliberately paradoxical. It does not ask you to add a new object called 'Brahman' to your inventory of knowns. It points to something that cannot be made an object at all—because it is the very knowing by which every object is known. Since truth cannot be placed before you as a known, self-inquiry removes false identifications—body, mind, sensations, emotions, concepts—not to arrive at a new remainder called Awareness, but to reveal what was never absent: the knower that cannot be objectified because it is objectification's own ground.

This is the precise distinction between neti-neti as elimination and neti-neti as recognition. You are not subtracting objects until a residual pure Awareness is left over. Awareness is not what remains after subtraction—it is what was always already present, perpetually overlooked because attention flowed outward toward what it knew, never inward toward what was knowing. Every negation is not an unveiling in the sense of producing something new; it is the removal of the obscuration that prevented recognition of what was never hidden.

### **1.2 Why Permanence Serves as Advaita's Criterion of Truth**

Understanding why neti-neti works requires grasping Advaita's criterion of truth. This is not arbitrary philosophical preference but a discovery emerging from phenomenological investigation.

According to Advaita, as articulated by Sankara following the Upanisads, truth (satya) is that which is unchanging, non-negatable, and constant across all states of experience. Objects—gross or subtle—are by nature impermanent, dependent, and intermittently present. Anything that appears at one time and disappears at another, that varies with conditions, or that is sublated in a different state of Awareness, cannot qualify as ultimately real.

The Upanisads themselves repeatedly point to this distinction. Waking objects are negated in dream—the solid world you inhabited moments ago vanishes completely when you fall asleep. Dream objects are negated in deep sleep—the vivid drama you were absorbed in dissolves into absence. Even subtle cognitions dissolve when their conditions cease. What remains constant? Only Awareness itself—the knowing by which change is recognized as change.

Advaita does not arbitrarily choose permanence as its criterion of truth; rather, permanence is discovered phenomenologically as the only feature common to all experience across all three states. Since every object is known as changing, it is disqualified from being absolutely real. What knows change without itself changing alone satisfies the Upanisadic requirement of truth.

A serious objection must be acknowledged here. The Buddhist tradition—Advaita's most rigorous philosophical interlocutor—disputes this entire framework. Buddhism argues that permanence is itself a conceptual projection onto momentary flux; that what Advaita calls 'the unchanging Witness' is precisely the kind of reified self that Buddhism's anatman doctrine dismantles. The debate between Advaita and Madhyamika Buddhism on this point is among the deepest in world philosophy. Advaita's response, most fully developed in Gaudapada's Mandukya Karika, is that the Buddhist denial of a permanent self still presupposes the awareness within which flux appears—denying the river does not eliminate the knowing of the river. But this response requires the reader's own investigation to verify, not merely philosophical acceptance. The criterion of permanence is offered not as dogma but as a hypothesis: look and see whether, beneath all changing contents, there is anything that remains constant. This chapter's invitation is to investigate rather than conclude.

### **1.3 The Direction of Investigation**

Ordinary attention moves outward—toward thoughts, perceptions, sensations, the world. You are absorbed in what is appearing: thinking about what happened, planning what will happen, reacting to what is happening now. The subject—what is aware of all this—remains uninvestigated, assumed, taken for granted. The seer is never seen; the knower is never known; attention flows continuously toward known objects while the knowing itself goes unexamined.

Self-inquiry reverses this movement. The investigation turns attention 180 degrees—from observed to observer, from content to context, from what appears to what is aware of the appearance. Instead of following thoughts outward into their content, you turn attention back: what is aware of these thoughts? Instead of being absorbed in sensations, you investigate: what knows these sensations are present?

You do not ask who am I psychologically but what is this I that I take myself to be. This is not psychological analysis but existential investigation. Not what are my characteristics but does the separate self I take myself to be actually exist as I imagine it.

These questions do not want an answer in the form of another concept. They want direct looking. This is empirical investigation in the deepest sense—examining what is directly observable in immediate experience. No belief in God, no faith in scripture, no dependence on divine grace. Just honest attention to what is actually here when you look.

Consider what this turning feels like. You are reading these words. Thoughts are arising as you read—associations, agreements, doubts. Normally, you follow those thoughts. Self-inquiry does something different: it asks, right now, what is aware of the fact that thoughts are arising? Not the content of the thoughts—the fact of their arising. Something knows they are arising. What is that knowing? Can you find it as a located thing, a bounded something? Or does the looking itself become the recognition—Awareness knowing itself, not as an object found but as the finding itself?

### **1.4 The Asymmetry Between Awareness and Its Contents**

The method uses a fundamental asymmetry in experience: Awareness knows all objects, but objects do not know Awareness. You are aware of thoughts, but thoughts are not aware of you. You know sensations are present, but sensations do not know you are present. This asymmetry reveals that Awareness and its contents are not the same kind of thing.

You cannot be what you observe because you are what is doing the observing. This is the principle of discrimination—viveka—that self-inquiry employs. The Drig-Drishya-Viveka systematically applies this method through its opening five verses: forms are seen by the eye; the eye is seen by the mind; the mind's modifications are illumined by Consciousness; that Consciousness does not rise and does not set, shines by itself, and illumines others without aid (DDV, Verses 1-5). Whatever is seen cannot be the seer; whatever is known cannot be the knower.

But—and this is crucial—this discrimination is not claiming two ontologically distinct substances exist eternally separate. It is using functional difference as an investigative tool to break the habitual identification that says I am this body, I am these thoughts, I am this person. The asymmetry functions as a pedagogical device to break habitual identification with the body-mind.

Once the device has done its work, it is transcended to reveal the fundamental unity between subject and objects as both being appearances of Awareness. This transcendence is not a later stage tacked on—it is already implicit in the asymmetry itself, for if objects have no awareness of their own, their apparent existence is entirely dependent on the Awareness that knows them. The step from discrimination to non-duality is already prepared in the method's own logic.

## **2\. The Two Classical Texts: Why These, and Why Now**

Every method has its sources. Every teacher arrived at their teaching through some path—a book encountered, a person met, a crisis that forced reckoning. This book draws its methodological foundation from two classical texts of the Advaita tradition: the Drig-Drishya-Viveka and Gaudapada's Mandukya Karika. It is worth being transparent about why these texts specifically, and in what sequence they entered this inquiry—because the reasons are not merely scholarly.

### **2.1 The Drig-Drishya-Viveka: What Krishnamurti Was Pointing Toward**

When Krishnamurti fell from a college library shelf at my feet—that improbable beginning described in the Preface—I had no idea I was encountering a preparation for something I would not encounter for another seventeen years. What Krishnamurti gave me was choiceless awareness: the capacity to observe without the observer's interference, to see thought without the thinker's distortion, to rest in bare perception without the commentary of the commentator. He called it awareness without a chooser. I practiced it as best I could, through daily life, through relationships, through the relentless movement of thought he tracked so precisely.

When I finally encountered the Drig-Drishya-Viveka, I recognized immediately what it was doing. Here was the same discrimination—between the seer and the seen, between the observer and the observed—but prosecuted with methodological precision that Krishnamurti never provided. Krishnamurti worked psychologically: he examined the movement of thought, the structure of the self, the way the thinker creates its own problems. The DDV worked phenomenologically: it investigated the nature of experience itself, asking what exactly is the witness of these thoughts.

The similarity is real and illuminating. The difference is decisive. Krishnamurti's observer dissolves into awareness when the psychological movement of thought is seen through—but he never named what that awareness is. The DDV takes the crucial step: it discriminates systematically between reflected consciousness (chidabhasa—Awareness as it appears through the mind) and pure Witnessing Awareness (sakshi—Consciousness that never rises or sets, that shines by itself). Krishnamurti stops where the DDV begins. His choiceless awareness clears the ground; Advaita points to the ground itself as your own nature.

So why choose the DDV as this book's methodological foundation for the discrimination stage? Because it approaches the question exactly where the contemporary secular seeker lives: in experience itself, in the structure of what is happening right now, without theological framework. The DDV never mentions Ishvara. It proceeds entirely through discrimination—this is seen, what sees it?—through twenty-nine verses that move systematically from gross to subtle, from form seen by eye, to eye seen by mind, to mind illumined by Consciousness that is never itself illumined by anything else. This is the investigative rigour that Krishnamurti pointed toward but never systematized.

### **2.2 Gaudapada's Mandukya Karika: What Greg Goode Pointed Toward**

Seven years after my own akhandakara vritti—described in the Preface—a problem remained. I had recognized myself as sakshi, the Witness, the eternal Awareness prior to all arising and disappearing phenomena. The existential terror had dissolved. But a subtle duality persisted: Awareness here as Witness, objects there as witnessed. Subject and object, observer and observed—even if the observer was now recognized as Awareness rather than as the body-mind, there were still two. In a teaching that insists on not-two, this would not do.

It was Greg Goode—patient, precise, philosophically uncompromising—who finally pointed me toward the Mandukya Upanisad and Gaudapada's Karika. He had seen through my confusion: I was working within the Direct Path pedagogy of Atmananda Krishna Menon, transmitted through teachers like Francis Lucille and Greg himself, but filtering it through the traditional Advaita framework of sakshi that Swartz had given me. The two pedagogies treat the Witness differently. The confusion would not resolve until I engaged Gaudapada directly.

When I read Gaudapada's exposition of ajativada—the doctrine of non-origination—everything clicked into place. Not gradually. Suddenly, completely, and without any possibility of going back. The Mandukya Karika's final teaching is this: nothing was ever created. Not really, and not even illusorily. The Witness position—Awareness here observing objects there—rests on an assumption that objects were ever really other than Awareness. When that assumption is dissolved by rigorous philosophical analysis, the Witness position collapses—not into void, but into the recognition that Awareness never required a Witness position at all. It was always already whole, undivided, the sole reality appearing as the entire display.

So why choose the Mandukya Karika as this book's philosophical terminus? Because it takes the DDV's discrimination to its logical conclusion. The DDV reveals that whatever is seen cannot be the seer. The Mandukya Karika asks the next question: but is there really a seer standing apart from what is seen? And when Gaudapada finishes with that question, neither the seen nor the seer remains as an independently standing reality. What remains is Awareness—unborn, undivided, exactly as it always was.

This combination—the DDV's direct experiential discrimination and the Mandukya Karika's uncompromising philosophical analysis—provides the methodological foundation for everything that follows in this book. Not because they are the only worthy texts in the Advaita tradition, but because they represent what this book's author found to work: a path that begins in direct experience, proceeds through rigorous discrimination, and arrives at the radical conclusion without requiring a single theological commitment along the way.

### **2.3 The Distinctive Integration of Dimensions**

What makes these classical texts distinctively suited to contemporary secular inquiry is how they integrate three dimensions that modern presentations tend to separate: direct experiential investigation, psychological analysis, and metaphysical teaching.

The direct experiential investigation shows you how to examine the structure of your own experience. Right now, what is actually happening? Perceptions arise, thoughts appear, sensations occur. All of this is known. What knows it? This is not philosophical theory but direct looking at what is immediately present.

The psychological analysis explains why this examination is difficult and necessary. The mind habitually identifies with contents of Awareness, creating the sense of being a separate self. This identification generates fear, desire, and the entire structure of psychological suffering. Understanding this mechanism—how identification operates, why it persists, what reinforces it—provides the psychological clarity about what needs to be seen through.

The metaphysical teaching articulates what is revealed when investigation succeeds. Not just that you are Awareness rather than the body-mind, but what that Awareness ultimately is: not personal consciousness belonging to an individual but Brahman itself, the sole reality appearing as all phenomena. This metaphysical precision prevents confusion—distinguishing Advaita from pantheism, from idealism, from Buddhist emptiness teachings.

Modern presentations often emphasize one dimension at the expense of others. Teachers influenced by Ramana Maharshi or Nisargadatta Maharaj focus primarily on direct experiential pointing, sometimes minimizing the philosophical and metaphysical dimensions. Their sophistication lies in pointing, not in systematic philosophical exposition. Western Direct Path teachers—influenced by Atmananda Krishna Menon and transmitted through Lucille, Spira, and Goode—offer experiential exploration and psychological accessibility, but sometimes at the cost of the metaphysical precision that prevents spiritual bypassing. Academic presentations treat Advaita as philosophy but miss the direct experiential investigation that gives it teeth.

The classical approach integrates all three. The DDV begins with direct experiential discrimination but quickly moves to psychological analysis of how identification with the seen creates bondage, and then to metaphysical teaching about what the seer ultimately is. Gaudapada's Karika provides metaphysical rigour grounded in analysis of direct experience—the three states—with psychological implications clearly articulated. This makes the approach both philosophically precise and practically effective. You understand not just what to do (discriminate between seer and seen) but why this works (psychological mechanism of identification) and what it reveals (metaphysical truth of non-dual Awareness).

It is important to note that the Advaita corpus is not monolithic in method. Some texts—most notably the Bhagavad Gita—deliberately combine a gradual, devotional-ethical approach with pointers toward non-dual insight, teaching in stages about Ishvara, creation, and the path of action before steering the mature student toward recognition. Other works—the DDV and the Mandukya with Gaudapada—lean wholly toward immediate, direct inquiry without dwelling on a creator figure. The tradition contains both pedagogies: the ladder of superimposition and negation for those who need it, and the uncompromising direct path for those ready to look without intermediary constructs. This book chooses the second—not dismissing the first, but recognizing that its readers have arrived at the second by temperament, by history, or by honest reckoning with what they can and cannot accept.

## **3\. Authority and Reasoning in the Advaita Tradition**

A tension runs through the entire Advaita tradition—a tension between shruti (the sacred text that claims highest authority) and yukti (the reasoning that tests every claim the text appears to make). Many readers misunderstand this, imagining Sankara as a scriptural fundamentalist, insisting that the Vedas are infallible and that one must simply bow down to them. Nothing could be further from the truth.

This tension is particularly relevant for understanding self-inquiry as presented here. Throughout this chapter, I cite Upanisadic statements while insisting self-inquiry is empirical investigation of direct experience. Why quote scripture if you can verify everything yourself? Why appeal to Sankara and Gaudapada if direct looking is sufficient?

The answer lies in understanding how Advaita navigates between blind faith (accepting scripture without investigation) and pure rationalism (rejecting all authority in favour of unfettered reason). Self-inquiry requires both: scripture provides direction for investigation—showing where to look and what to look for—while reason verifies that what scripture points to is indeed what is revealed in direct experience. Neither alone suffices; together they prevent the twin errors of dogmatic belief and endless philosophical speculation.

### **3.1 Shruti as the Means of Knowledge (Pramana)**

Sankara was not defending blind submission. He was defending a very specific use of reasoning, one tethered to the Upanisads. He had observed that free-floating reasoning—reasoning not rooted in shruti—led to endless philosophical conflict. Each rival school (Naiyayikas, Samkhyas, Vaisesikas, Mimamsakas, Bauddhas) constructed its own logical, apparently airtight system, and each system contradicted all the others, arriving at wildly different visions of reality. Reason alone was not the problem. Ungoverned reason was.

This is the profound context behind Sankara's insistence on scriptural authority. It was not the authority of dogma but the authority of a verified phenomenology, preserved by sages who had directly realized what they taught. Scripture is not a set of beliefs to accept; it is the pramana—the means of knowledge—for knowing the Self, which cannot be known through perception or inference alone.

It is striking that the very first sutra of the Brahmasutra—athato brahma jijnasa (1.1.1)—Sankara glosses by saying that Brahman cannot be known through any means other than the Upanisads. Not because the Upanisads demand obedience, but because Brahman, being non-objectifiable, is not available to the senses or to inference. Therefore, reasoning, Sankara insists in his Brahmasutra Bhashya (1.1.2), must operate in alignment with scripture—upapatti-sahitam shruti-mulam eva jnanam—knowledge grounded in scripture and supported by reasoning.

The logic here is subtle. The Upanisads speak from the standpoint of direct realization. Reasoning operates within the boundaries set by that realization—not to confine thinking, but to prevent thinking from creating its own fantasies.

Scripture's authority in self-inquiry is not doctrinal but methodological. The Upanisads do not ask you to believe that 'you are Brahman'—they direct investigation: look for the separate self and see whether it can be found. Their authority lies in accurately describing what direct investigation reveals, not in commanding assent to propositions. They function as phenomenological reports from those who have completed the investigation, showing where to look and what to look for—not what to believe.

Here is where the crucial clarification for the secular reader must be made. The three traditional means of valid knowledge in Advaita are shruti (scriptural testimony), yukti (reasoning), and anubhava (direct experience). For knowing Brahman, Sankara argues that shruti is primary because Brahman cannot be objectified by the other two means alone. But anubhava—direct recognition, aparoksha-anubhuti, immediate non-inferential knowing—is itself the final pramana, the one that settles the question. Scripture and reason serve as preparatory means; direct recognition completes the inquiry. This is why the approach in this book is not a contradiction: it uses scripture as a methodological guide, uses reasoning to verify consistency, but stakes everything on anubhava—on what you actually find when you look. The secular reader who cannot accept scriptural authority is still invited to follow the investigation as a hypothesis to test: look and see whether what Gaudapada and the DDV describe is verified in direct experience. The authority of the texts is ultimately the authority of their descriptions' accuracy—which you test yourself.

### **3.2 The Without-God Approach: Why These Texts Specifically**

This raises an important question for the without-God approach that runs through this book: if Advaita scriptures often speak of Isvara (God as cosmic creator), and if this teaching relies on scriptural authority, how can self-inquiry proceed without theistic commitments?

The answer lies in understanding the pedagogical structure. Chapter 3 detailed the method of adhyaropa-apavada (superimposition and negation), showing how Advaita builds conceptual frameworks only to systematically dismantle them. When the Upanisads speak of Isvara creating the universe, this belongs to adhyaropa—the superimposition phase, the lower rung of the teaching ladder. It is provisional instruction for those who need a creation framework. Verses that describe creation (As from a fire set ablaze, sparks by the thousands fly forth—Mundaka Upanisad 2.1.1; or Brahman desired: so'kamayata bahu syam prajayeyeti—Taittiriya Upanisad 2.6.1) explain the world to beginners.

But these same texts then negate creation entirely in passages like: There is no diversity here whatsoever (Brhadaranyaka Upanisad 4.4.19); or ekam eva advitiyam—the One alone, without a second (Chandogya Upanisad 6.2.1). The ultimate teaching—apavada, negation—requires no God-concept. When you investigate directly and find no separate self, no creation, only Awareness appearing as multiplicity, you have verified the negation passages. The creation passages served their pedagogical purpose but are transcended in direct recognition.

This is why self-inquiry can draw on scriptural methodology while requiring no theistic belief—you are using the ladder (including its theistic rungs) while recognizing those rungs as provisional supports, not ultimate truth.

The without-God approach does not mean without scripture—it means without requiring theistic commitments that cannot be verified. The DDV and Gaudapada's Mandukya Karika represent Advaita's direct inquiry strand precisely because they do not build on God-concepts even provisionally.

The DDV never mentions Ishvara. It proceeds entirely through discrimination: this is seen, what sees it? This iterative investigation requires no theological framework—only willingness to examine the structure of experience itself.

Gaudapada's Karika explicitly critiques devotional approaches. Verse 3.1 states: The Jiva betaking itself to devotion (upasana) thinks itself to be related to the Brahman that is supposed to have manifested Himself. He is said to be of narrow intellect because he thinks that before creation all was of the nature of the unborn (Reality). This is not casual dismissal but recognition that the devotional framework preserves duality—devotee and deity—incompatible with ajativada's uncompromising non-dualism.

So when this book appeals to scriptural authority, it is appealing specifically to texts that do not require theistic belief even at preliminary stages. You are following the direct inquiry strand that Gaudapada himself distinguished from devotional methods. This is not cherry-picking—it is recognizing that the Advaita tradition contains multiple pedagogies and explicitly choosing the one that requires no God-concepts from start to finish.

### **3.3 Gaudapada and the Convergence of Reason**

The Mandukya Upanisad and Gaudapada's Karika provide the razor's edge of this method. The Mandukya first meticulously describes the waking, dream, and deep sleep states. Then the eighth mantra detonates this structure: the fourth is nothing perceivable, the cessation of all phenomena. Creation disappears.

Gaudapada then pushes reason to its limit, concluding that all reasoning ends in ajati—non-origination. This is not irrationality but the point where reason, fully exercised, recognizes what is beyond it. Crucially, Gaudapada affirms the authority of scripture, yet insists on inquiry (Mandukya Karika 3.23): That which is supported by Shruti and corroborated by reason, is alone true and not the other. Scripture reveals what cannot be known otherwise; reasoning confirms that nothing contradicts this revelation.

The deeper point is this: Advaita's reliance on scripture is not a crutch; it is a guardrail. Reason, untethered, proliferates worlds. Scripture, untethered, descends into dogma. Reasoning that contradicts scripture is errant. Scripture that contradicts reasoning is misunderstood. When the two align, realization is near.

This is not blind faith. It is the discipline of seeing. When the Upanisads say neti-neti, they are not demanding belief in a doctrine—they are directing an investigation. When Gaudapada deconstructs all causation, he is not proposing a metaphysical theory—he is showing what sustained reasoning reveals. Scripture provides the map; reason confirms the terrain matches; direct recognition completes the journey.

## **Conclusion: Foundation for What Follows**

We have covered significant territory: what self-inquiry is and is not, the two classical texts that form this book's methodological foundation and why they were chosen, and the epistemological relationship between scripture, reason, and direct experience.

But all of this serves one purpose: directing you toward direct investigation of what you are. Not to give you knowledge about Awareness, but to prompt recognition as Awareness.

The classical texts, the philosophical precision, the historical grounding—none of this matters if you do not actually look. The entire edifice of Advaita exists only to point you toward this looking.

Chapter 6 will now detail the four stages through which this investigation actually unfolds in practice—from the preparatory dismantling of Chapter 4 through the shravana-manana-nididhyasana triad, through Witness recognition, to the dissolution of the Witness itself in ajativada. But the looking can happen now.

The question stands—naked, urgent, genuine: What am I?

Not the body (that is observed). Not thoughts (those come and go). Not emotions (those change). Not the person (that is an appearance in Awareness).

Then what?

Look.
